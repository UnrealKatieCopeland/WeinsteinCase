1. Nonrenewal and Lease Expiration

Representative Language

“The non renewal provided to you on August 26, 2025 was appropriate—and your lease expired on October 26, 2025.”

The communication treats the August 26 nonrenewal and October 26, 2025 date as ending all ongoing obligations, notwithstanding prior disability disclosures and mold-related findings that rendered the unit unsafe.

2. November 2025 Notice to Vacate and Rent

Representative Language

“The Notice to Vacate, issued in November, was also appropriate.”

Plaintiffs reaffirm the validity of the November Notice to Vacate and associated rent demand for a period during which the unit had already been professionally determined unfit for human occupancy and Defendant was displaced.

3. Access to Medical Items and Devices

Representative Language

“[Medical items]… please identify those with specificity… once identified—arrangements will be made to deliver those to your present location.”

The email indicates that medically necessary items will be delivered once they are specifically identified, but does not address the impact of delay on a disabled tenant’s health or the requirement that essential medication and equipment be provided promptly as part of a reasonable accommodation.

4. Characterization of the Court’s Rulings

Representative Language

“The Court did not comment on the validity of the claims, nor on your assertion that you continue to have a valid lease…”

Plaintiffs frame the Court’s statements as limited to the special exceptions and motion to quash, and take the position that there is no present obligation to alter conduct in response to federal or state disability law until there is a final merits ruling.

Net Effect (Column A): The December 4 email treats the lease as terminated, rent as fully collectible, and accommodations as discretionary, despite prior disability notice and a unit deemed unsafe, signaling continued noncompliance absent clear court direction.

1. Retaliatory Nonrenewal and Continuing Tenancy

  • Tex. Prop. Code §§ 92.331–92.335 create a presumption of retaliation where adverse actions (such as a nonrenewal) follow a tenant’s good-faith complaints or exercise of rights within six months.
  • When the presumption is not rebutted, a retaliatory termination is not enforceable, and the tenancy is treated as continuing for purposes of the statute, including protections against eviction and adverse treatment.

2. Habitability, Repairs, and Rent During Uninhabitable Periods

  • Tex. Prop. Code § 92.052 imposes a non-waivable duty on landlords to diligently repair conditions that materially affect an ordinary tenant’s physical health or safety once proper notice is provided.
  • When a unit has been professionally determined unsafe for occupancy, ongoing rent demands for that period are in tension with both habitability and anti-retaliation provisions, particularly where the tenant has been displaced.

3. Fair Housing Act / Texas Fair Housing Act – Disability Accommodations

  • Under 42 U.S.C. § 3604(f)(3)(B) and Tex. Prop. Code § 301.025, housing providers must make reasonable accommodations in rules, policies, practices, or services when necessary to afford a person with a disability an equal opportunity to use and enjoy a dwelling.
  • Timely access to medications and medical devices is typically treated as part of an effective accommodation when those items are necessary for the tenant’s health and continued participation in daily life.
  • The anti-interference provision, 42 U.S.C. § 3617, prohibits coercive or conditional treatment tied to the exercise of fair housing rights, including requests for repairs or accommodations.

4. Court’s Role in Enforcing Non-Waivable Statutory Duties

  • Federal and state disability protections, as well as Texas habitability and anti-retaliation statutes, impose duties that cannot be contracted away or ignored based solely on lease end-dates.
  • A clarifying order confirms that nothing in any scheduling order, discovery ruling, or interim directive authorizes noncompliance with these statutes, and that all parties must align their conduct with the governing law while the case proceeds.

Net Effect (Column B): The governing statutes treat disability accommodations, retaliation limits, and habitability duties as non-waivable, and support a clarifying order that requires ongoing compliance regardless of Plaintiffs’ lease-expiration theory.
