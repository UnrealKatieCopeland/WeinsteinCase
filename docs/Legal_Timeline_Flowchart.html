üè† Step 1 ‚Äì Hazard Confirmed
What should have happened
Unit condemned ‚Üí taken out of service

Once a licensed mold assessor or inspector finds a unit unfit for human occupancy due to mold and moisture, the unit should be taken out of service.

The landlord and its insurer should accept that the unit is unsafe and plan around that reality, including relocation, licensed remediation, and safe handling of contents.

What actually happened
Hazard acknowledged, then treated as temporary

My unit was professionally deemed unfit for human occupancy.

Weinstein and its insurer acknowledged serious issues, but instead of treating the unit as truly out of service and planning for safe, ongoing housing, they treated the situation as temporary.

They cut off housing entirely on October 27, 2025, while contamination remained unresolved. This set the stage for constructive eviction and left a disabled tenant without any safe place to live.

‚ôø Step 2 ‚Äì Disability & Accommodation Request
What should have happened
Interactive process under FHA & ADA

When a disabled tenant notifies a housing provider that the unit is unsafe and requests help, that request must be treated as a reasonable accommodation request under the Fair Housing Act and Texas Fair Housing Act.

The provider should engage in a genuine interactive process to identify a reasonable accommodation, including exploring relocation, extended stays, or comparable safe housing.

What actually happened
Accommodation request treated as a nuisance

I repeatedly informed Weinstein of my disabilities and the medical impact of the mold, and I provided supporting documentation.

Instead of engaging in a meaningful interactive process, Weinstein moved toward non-renewal, litigation, and ultimately cutting off housing.

A request for safe housing in this context is not a ‚Äúfavor‚Äù ‚Äì it is a request for a reasonable accommodation. Ignoring or resisting that request is an ongoing fair-housing problem, not just a lease issue.

üè® Step 3 ‚Äì Temporary Housing
What should have happened
Continuity of safe housing

When a unit is uninhabitable for a disabled tenant, a reasonable accommodation can include medically safe, ADA-compliant, comparable temporary housing‚Äîsuch as a furnished extended-stay that allows lying flat, managing symptoms, basic kitchen access, and accommodation of children and assistance animals.

Temporary housing should continue while the original unit remains unfit or until genuinely comparable, safe housing is offered.

What actually happened
Housing support cut off midstream

Tokio arranged temporary housing for a period, recognizing my unit was not livable.

On October 27, 2025, that housing was cut off even though the original unit remained unfit and remediation/contents issues were unresolved.

Since then, I have been effectively homeless, despite ongoing disability-related needs. Cutting off housing midstream, while the original unit is still unsafe, is the opposite of a reasonable accommodation and turns a partial attempt to comply into an ongoing failure to accommodate.

üì¶ Step 4 ‚Äì Contents & Mold Compliance
What should have happened
Licensed, transparent handling of belongings

Mold protocols must be carried out by or under the supervision of properly licensed mold remediation professionals.

A tenant's belongings should be inventoried, triaged (salvageable vs. non-salvageable), and handled consistent with the protocol and state law, with clear communication to the tenant.

What actually happened
Unlicensed handling & risk of spoliation

Most of my belongings were handed to Reign Restoration, which does not appear to hold a Texas mold remediation license, despite a protocol requiring licensed professionals.

I have no complete inventory, no clear explanation of what has been done to my contents under the protocol, and no timeline for licensed compliance or safe retrieval/decision-making.

My property and evidence are at continuing risk of spoliation and mishandling, and the protocol Plaintiffs rely on is not being followed in a way that complies with Texas law.

‚öñÔ∏è Step 5 ‚Äì Use of the Courts
What should have happened
Courts as enforcers of non-waivable rights

Courts should not be used as a vehicle to sidestep non-waivable statutory duties under the FHA, TFHA, ADA, ¬ß 504, or Texas Property Code.

Requests for rent, possession, and scheduling should account for a landlord's fair-housing obligations to a disabled tenant from an uninhabitable unit and should ensure accommodations, not punish the tenant for asserting rights.

What actually happened
Leverage built on ignoring accommodation duties

While these federal and state disability and fair-housing duties have been repeatedly raised, Plaintiffs continue to seek possession, rent, and scheduling orders as if this were a simple lease dispute.

My motions for accommodations, referral to enforcement authorities, and appointment of counsel have not yet been addressed, while I remain without stable, medically appropriate housing.

This creates the risk that the Court will be used to enforce leverage that depends on ignoring fair-housing and disability obligations rather than ensuring they are honored.
